How to Support a Friend With Cancer

D
Devin The Vibe Check Project

When someone you love tells you they have cancer, the ground shifts beneath you. Your brain scrambles for the right response. "I'm so sorry" feels empty. "You'll beat this" feels presumptuous. Silence feels like abandonment.

Here's what nobody tells you: there is no script for this. But there ARE things that help — and things that hurt, even with good intentions. This guide is honest about both.

What to Say

"I'm here. Tell me what you need — or don't."

The most powerful first response is presence without pressure. Don't force optimism. Don't demand details. Just show up.

"I don't know the right thing to say, so I'll just say this: I'm here. You don't have to update me, perform bravery, or make me feel better about YOUR diagnosis. I just want you to know you're not doing this alone."

"Can I handle [specific thing] for you this week?"

"Let me know if you need anything" is well-meaning but useless. People battling cancer don't have the energy to assign tasks. Take initiative.

"I'm going to pick up your groceries every Thursday. Text me your list by Wednesday night. If you don't text me, I'll get the usual. This isn't up for debate."

"What does a good day look like for you right now?"

This question meets them where they ARE, not where you want them to be. Their definition of a "good day" might be drastically different now. Asking shows you understand that.

What to DO

  • Show up for treatment days. Chemo is boring, exhausting, and lonely. Sitting in the waiting room, driving them home, bringing a blanket — these are the acts people remember forever.
  • Send things that aren't flowers. Cozy socks. A good audiobook. A meal delivery gift card. A basket of their favorite snacks. Practical > pretty.
  • Be normal. Talk about the game. Send memes. Gossip about mutual friends. Not every interaction needs to be about cancer. Sometimes the best gift is 30 minutes of normalcy.
  • Set calendar reminders. The initial wave of support fades after a few weeks. Set a recurring reminder to check in every Sunday. Be the friend who doesn't disappear.
  • Support their caregiver too. The partner, parent, or sibling managing appointments, medication, and emotional labor is drowning too. Bring THEM food. Ask how THEY'RE doing.

What NOT to Say

  • "Everything happens for a reason." — There is no reason for cancer. This is the single most hurtful sentence cancer patients report hearing.
  • "You're so BRAVE." — They didn't choose this. Being sick isn't a character trait. It's a battle they didn't volunteer for.
  • "My aunt had the same thing and she..." — Unless asked, don't share other cancer stories. Especially ones that ended badly.
  • "Have you tried [alternative treatment]?" — They have an oncologist. Trust their medical team.
  • "You look great!" — This can feel dismissive of their internal reality. They might look "fine" and feel terrible.
  • Ghosting. — People who disappear because they "don't know what to say" cause real, lasting hurt. A clumsy text is infinitely better than silence.

The Long Haul

Cancer treatment isn't a sprint. It's months — sometimes years — of appointments, side effects, anxiety, and uncertainty. The best friends are the ones who pace themselves for the marathon.

You won't always get it right. You'll say the wrong thing sometimes. You'll forget to call. That's okay. What matters is that you keep showing up — imperfectly, consistently, honestly.

Your friend doesn't need you to be perfect. They need you to be present. That is enough.
